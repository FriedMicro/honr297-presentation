Glacial Formations

Present mostly in polar regions, glaciers cover 10% of the world’s surface. Most are the product of the Quaternary glaciation over the past 100,000 years. They form through a process called nivation where snow remains on the ground year-round and accumulates, compressing past layers into tightly-packed ice. Through variations in the freeze-thaw cycle, glaciers can creep or change form over the course of their existence. Depending on their environment, glaciers can take many forms.

Types of Glaciers

Hanging Glaciers

Larger glacial systems can experience retreat at various rates. If a lower-elevation glacier retreats to or beneath a glacier that formed later, a hanging glacier is left in its stead.

Arêtes

When two or more glaciers form in close proximity, their creep can erode adjacent mountain faces. If such a face is surrounded on multiple sides by glacial formations, an arête is formed.

Cirques

Formed in a circular basin as the name suggests, cirques erode the landscape to create a glacial valley and commonly coincide with others to form aretes.

Ice Fields

Wide expanses of permafrost facilitate the development of ice fields, which flow very slowly and cover a large area of flat terrain.
